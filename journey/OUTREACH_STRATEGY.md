# Outreach Strategy Playbook

> "Don't ask for permission to add value. Just add it, then show up with the receipt."

This is the reference document for Doug's outreach approach. Not a log entry — a living playbook to execute against.

---

## Core Philosophy

**Demonstrate, don't apply.** Every other candidate sends a CV and says "I'm interested." Doug shows up having already done the work — a growth audit, a code contribution, a micro-demo, an article. The hiring manager's reaction should be "we need this person" before Doug even asks for anything.

## The Seven Approaches

### 1. Growth Audits as Cold Outreach

**What:** 30-minute teardown of a target company's public-facing product. Website performance, SEO, conversion flows, onboarding, pricing page, referral mechanics. Delivered as a 1-page PDF or 2-minute Loom video.

**Why it works:** It proves you can add value on day one. A hiring manager reading "I'd improve your onboarding" on a CV doesn't believe it. A hiring manager seeing a specific, insightful audit of *their* product does.

**Effort:** 30 min per company. High ROI for top targets.

**Best targets:**
- **ElevenLabs** (Growth Engineer role) — Audit their pricing page, API dev experience, conversion funnel. Show growth thinking before they ask
- **Tractive** — App store listing, onboarding flow, referral mechanics. "3 experiments I'd run in my first week"
- **Realm.fun** — They're bootstrapped. SEO, landing page conversion, pricing tiers. Small companies feel this immediately
- **Deezer** — Web player experience vs Spotify. Where's the gap?
- **Arbio** — Holiday rental landing pages, SEO, booking conversion vs Airbnb

**Format:** Single-page PDF with:
- 3 specific observations (not generic — reference actual pages/flows)
- 1 quick win (something fixable in a week)
- 1 bigger opportunity (something that needs a quarter)
- "Here's what I'd build in the first 90 days" closing

**Template:** Create a repeatable format so each audit takes 30 min, not 3 hours.

### 2. The Article: This Job Search IS the Portfolio Piece

**What:** Write up the entire job search system as a narrative article. "I Built an AI-Powered System to Research 162 Companies in a Day — Here's What I Learned."

**Why it works:** It's meta — a growth engineering project about finding a growth engineering job, built with the tools and thinking a growth engineer would use. It's the proof and the pitch in one artifact. Published on HN/Dev.to, it reaches all 162 companies at once.

**Effort:** Medium. The journey log is 80% of the content already. Needs packaging and narrative polish.

**Content beats:**
1. The problem: job searching is broken — you're swiping LinkedIn blind
2. The build: AI agents for company research, automated reputation scoring, custom review tool
3. The data: what 162 companies taught us about the European startup landscape
4. The insight: Glassdoor scores lie (with specific examples — Bending Spoons 4.7 but fires everyone they acquire)
5. The build-vs-buy moment: replaced two SaaS services with 50 lines of Puppeteer
6. The agency angle: an unexpected strategic insight that changed the search direction
7. The meta: using growth engineering to find a growth engineering job

**Publish to:** Personal blog (SEO), HN (reach), Dev.to (developer audience), LinkedIn (professional network), Twitter thread (viral potential).

**Timing:** Before outreach begins. Let the article create inbound interest first.

### 3. Micro-Demos Targeting Specific Companies

**What:** 2-4 hour builds that demonstrate domain understanding for specific targets. Not full products — focused experiments.

**Why it works:** Shows you understand their world AND can ship. An agency hiring manager seeing a creative Three.js piece built in their style thinks "this person gets us."

**Ideas by target type:**

| Target | Demo Idea | Effort |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **14islands / Bakken & Bæck** | Creative Three.js web experiment — interactive scroll, particle system, something visually striking. Post on Twitter, tag them | 3-4 hrs |
| **ElevenLabs** | Voice-powered interactive web experience using their API. A creative toy that shows product understanding + web engineering | 3-4 hrs |
| **Realm.fun / gaming** | The Source 2 modding platform IS the demo. Ship it, open-source it, write about it | Already building |
| **MapTiler** | Build something cool with their TypeScript SDK — an interactive map experience, a location-based game, a creative visualization | 2-3 hrs |
| **Lightdash** | Skip the demo, contribute a PR directly (see Approach 4) | 2-4 hrs |

**Rules:**
- Max 4 hours per demo. It's a signal, not a product
- Must be publicly visible (GitHub, Twitter, personal site)
- Must reference the target company's tech/domain naturally — not forced
- Quality > quantity. One great demo > five rushed ones

### 4. Open-Source Contribution Play

**What:** Contribute to open-source projects owned by target companies. Then apply as someone who's already shipped code in their codebase.

**Why it works:** You go from "random applicant" to "person who already contributed." It's the strongest possible signal of fit.

**Targets with open-source codebases:**
- **Lightdash** — Fully open-source BI platform (TypeScript, React, Node, Express). Perfect stack match. Find a meaningful issue, ship a PR
- **Apify** — Open-source crawling/scraping tools. Doug's specialty. Contributing here is almost too natural
- **MapTiler** — Open-source SDKs on GitHub. TypeScript-based

**Rules:**
- One meaningful PR > ten trivial ones. Fix a real bug, add a real feature, improve docs substantially
- Read their contributing guide. Follow their code style. Don't make extra work for maintainers
- After the PR is merged (or at least reviewed), apply referencing it

### 5. The "Forward Deployed Engineer" Pitch

**What:** Position the withSeismic career narrative as a "Forward Deployed Engineer" — someone who parachutes into the hardest problems and ships solutions.

**Why it works:** This is a real and growing role type. Doug's career IS this: Contra, Sky, Groupon, MIT, Framer — every engagement was "understand the problem, build the thing, deliver the outcome."

**Best for:** Agencies (ustwo, Bakken & Bæck, Brainhub, Stink Studios) and startups that need someone who can do client-facing technical work.

**Narrative structure:**
- "In 8 years of consultancy, I've deployed into 20+ client problems across media, gaming, education, and e-commerce"
- Specific examples with outcomes: Contra (Chrome extension saving 48hrs/week, ROI in 1 week), MIT (50,000 students), Patrianna (0→1M players)
- "I don't wait to be told what to build. I find the problem, ship the fix, and measure the outcome"

### 6. Speculative "What I'd Build" Documents

**What:** For top 3-5 targets, write a 1-pager: "If I joined [Company] as Lead Engineer, here's what I'd build in the first 90 days."

**Why it works:** Shows research depth, outcome thinking, and opinions. Most candidates wait to be told what to do. This shows you already know.

**Structure per document:**
1. **Week 1-2:** Quick wins — things visible in the product within days
2. **Month 1:** Foundation — what infrastructure/processes need attention
3. **Month 2-3:** Growth moves — experiments, features, or systems that drive measurable impact
4. **What I'd measure:** Specific metrics tied to each initiative

**Rules:**
- Reference their actual product, actual gaps, actual tech stack
- Be specific enough to be useful, not so specific you're doing free consulting
- Show you understand their business model, not just their tech
- Max 1 page. Respect their time

### 7. Growth Teardown Livestream (Far-Fetched / High Risk)

**What:** Pick 5 companies from the list. Do a live teardown of their growth funnels — website, onboarding, pricing, SEO. Stream on YouTube/Twitter.

**Why it works:** If done well, establishes Doug as THE growth engineering voice in European startups. Companies come to him. It's the ultimate inbound play.

**Risks:** Comes across as arrogant if done poorly. Must be respectful, constructive, and demonstrate genuine expertise — not "look how bad their website is."

**Prerequisites:** Article published first (establishes context). At least one successful growth audit delivered privately first (validates the format works).

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

- **Don't spray-and-pray CVs** — 162 generic applications = 162 rejections
- **Don't lead with "I'm looking for a job"** — lead with value, context comes later
- **Don't spend 3 hours on an audit** — 30 minutes. It's a signal, not a deliverable
- **Don't build demos for companies with low fit scores** — focus energy on the 4/5 and 5/5 targets
- **Don't over-optimize the CV** — the audit/demo/article IS the application. The CV is just the receipt
- **Don't wait until everything is perfect** — ship the article, ship the audits, iterate based on responses
