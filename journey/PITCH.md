# The Pitch: Why Hire a Generalist Who's Done Everything Small

## The Pushback You'll Get

"Your experience is broad but nothing at scale."
"These companies are small — how do we know you can operate in a real engineering org?"
"You're a generalist. We need a specialist."

## The Reframe

Most senior engineers have done one thing at one company for four years. I've built, shipped, and sold three products. That's not a weakness. That's the point.

## What I've Actually Done

None of this was big. All of it was real.

- **3 commercial exits.** Promptheus (30K users, sold). Vouchernaut (250K monthly sessions, 100K+ brands, $100K ARR, sold 2021). getBenson (25M monthly sessions, 500+ stores, exited 2023). Small numbers. Real money. Real buyers.
- **Zero to 1M players.** Built and shipped 5 social casino games at Patrianna. Hired and led 9 people. Greenfield to production to scale. The company wasn't a household name. The results were.
- **#1 Product Hunt of the week.** Contra's Indy.ai Chrome extension. 35K+ users. Doubled marketplace inventory. Built the whole thing — LLM workflows, browser automation, the lot.
- **Clients who came back.** Contra, Framer, Sky, Motley Fool, MIT. Not because I'm cheap. Because I ship and they don't have to manage me.

## The Honest Version

I haven't worked at a FAANG. I haven't operated at 10,000 RPS. I haven't managed a 50-person org.

What I have done is walk into ambiguous situations — no team, no architecture, sometimes no product — and build the thing that made money. Repeatedly.

## Why That's What You Want

You're not hiring for a company that has everything figured out. If you were, you wouldn't be reading this.

You want someone who can:
- **Own a problem end-to-end.** Not wait for a spec. Not file a ticket for the backend. Not ask PM what to build next.
- **Ship fast and fix later.** I've built MVPs in weeks that became real businesses. I know the difference between "move fast" and "be reckless."
- **Hire and lead when it's time.** I've built a team from zero. I know when to do that and when to keep building myself.
- **Work across the stack without drama.** TypeScript, React, Node, Python, infrastructure, browser extensions, scraping, AI agents, growth systems. I'm not an expert in any one of these. I'm dangerous in all of them.

The specialist will go deeper on one thing. I'll find the thing that matters and build it before they've finished their design doc.

## The Elevator Version

15 years. 3 exits. Full-stack generalist. I've built products from zero, shipped them, scaled them, sold them. Small companies, real outcomes. I own problems, not tickets.

## The One-Liner

I build things that make money. Three times I've built them well enough that someone bought them.

## Where I Actually Fit

The job titles that describe what I do:
- **Founding Engineer** — done it twice, both exited
- **Product Engineer / Lead** — own the problem, not just the code
- **Head of Engineering** — built and led teams, set technical direction
- **Growth Engineer** — rare title, but exactly what I did at Patrianna (zero to 1M)
- **Lead Full-Stack Engineer** — the generalist who ships

The titles that don't fit, even though I could do the job:
- "Senior Frontend Engineer" — too narrow
- "Senior Backend Engineer" — too narrow
- "Staff Software Engineer" at a big company — different game entirely

## For Interviews: Handling "But It's Small"

**"Your companies weren't that big."**
"No, they weren't. That's why I built everything. At a big company that's six teams. I was all six. Three of those products got acquired."

**"How do we know you can work in a larger org?"**
"I've embedded inside client teams at Sky, Motley Fool, Contra, Framer. I know how to operate in someone else's codebase, someone else's process, someone else's culture. I just also know how to build the thing from scratch when there is no process."

**"You're a generalist — we need depth."**
"My depth is in shipping. I've taken more products from zero to revenue than most engineers will in their career. If you need someone to go deep on one service for two years, I'm not your person. If you need someone to figure out what to build and then build it, I am."

**"What's your biggest technical achievement?"**
"Contra's Chrome extension. 35,000 users, #1 Product Hunt, doubled their marketplace inventory. Built the entire thing — LLM integration, browser automation, data pipeline, the UI. Shipped it with a team of me."

## The Real Talk

The imposter syndrome is this: everything I've built has been small. The counter is this: I built all of it. The whole thing. And I sold three of them.

Most people at big companies built a feature. I built the company. The scale was small. The scope was everything.

That's not a gap. That's the selling point.
